The Places & Structures in Intramuros

Rizal Park


During Spanish rule, Rizal Park, formerly called Luneta, was important as a meeting place for Manila's elite and as a place where rebels were put to death. The execution of Jose Rizal in 1896 was a turning point in its history. In 1902, the architect and urban planner Daniel Burnham declared Luneta to be the center of the American government. His designs included notable buildings such as the Agricultural Building (now the National Museum of Natural History), Finance Building (now the National Museum of the Filipino People), and Senate Building (now the National Art Gallery). In 1913, the park was renamed Rizal Park in honor of Dr. Jose Rizal after a monument honoring him was built. It still serves as a symbol of bravery and independence, serving as a venue for political protests, national gatherings, and regular events like walks, concerts, jogging, and family picnics. The park endures despite people coming and departing, safeguarding monuments and memories that will guarantee Rizal Park's survival for generations to come.

Anda Circle


The Anda Monument, an obelisk marking the border between Intramuros and Port Area in central Manila, Philippines, was first built in honor of Simón de Anda y Salazar. It acts as an interchange system connecting Bonifacio Drive, Mel Lopez Boulevard, Andres Soriano Avenue (formerly Calle Aduana), and Roberto Oca Street. It is frequently mistakenly called the Anda Circle after the nearby intersections. The monument was built for the first time in 1871 close to the Pasig River as a way to publicly thank Anda for leading resistance against the British during Manila's occupation in 1762. Regretfully, Manila suffered severe damage during the Japanese occupation during World War II. After the war, the monument was redesigned as a monument circle and moved to its current location at the rotunda. In his speech on June 8, 1957, President Carlos P. Garcia honored Anda for his bravery and defense of the Filipino people against Spanish mistreatment.

Philippine National Library


The Philippine National Library is a government-funded service-oriented organization that houses a variety of Filipiniana collections, including rare books, historical accounts, and works written by Filipino authors. Its goal is for the Filipino people to fully develop intellectually and culturally via a love of reading all kinds of library books, from which they can get knowledge and understanding that will help them become more adaptable to changes in the global technology landscape. The National Library of the Philippines seeks to house the nation's printed and recorded cultural heritage, and other scholarly literary and information sources will make these materials accessible to the public for enlightenment, lifelong learning, civic engagement, and intellectual development.

Manila City Hall


One of the most notable landmarks in Manila, the Philippine capital, is Manila City Hall. The building, which has a hexagonal tower with three red-faced clocks on three of its sides, was criticized in its early years for its lack of entrances, minimalist architectural style, and clock tower placement. The floor layout of the building has been written off as having a shape comparable to a coffin or, conversely, the Knights Templar shield. For the same reasons that made the structure so condemned, many critics today admire the City Hall's design. The building is situated in the heart of a tourist region that is home to important government structures and famous sites. Situated just south of the city hall are the Department of Tourism, the Museum of the Filipino People, and the National Museum. Located south of the museums lies Rizal Park, the biggest urban park in the nation. Additionally, the walled city of Intramuros, the oldest and most historic area of Manila, is not far from the city hall.

Minor Basilica of San Sebastian


One of the nation's National Historical Landmarks and National Cultural Treasures is the Minor Basilica of San Sebastian, also referred to as the San Sebastian Basilica. Its distinctive appearance is primarily influenced by Gothic revival building design. It is also referred to as the only steel-constructed church in the nation. The San Sebastian Basilica, with its gothic-inspired architectural design, has demonstrated its timeless beauty for centuries since the church's construction. You will notice the church's enforcing foundations as soon as you walk in: the ceilings, pillars, and old chandeliers. The church has a distinct personality. Its custom tiles, ornately wrought metal designs, stained-glass windows, and ceiling paintings represent a plethora of diverse cultural influences. Even during the building of the San Sebastian Basilica, it was acknowledged as one of the important buildings in the nation. You won't want to miss the San Sebastian Basilica, which is a site of intricate beauty and a place of devotion as well as culture.

Fort Santiago


Fort Santiago is constructed in 1571 by Spanish navigator Miguel López de Legazpi in Manila. It is a significant citadel within the walled city of Intramuros. This historical site played a crucial role during Spanish rule and World War II, serving as a prison where notable figures, including Philippine Hero Jose Rizal, were held. The Rizal Shrine within the fort houses memorabilia of the hero, showcasing his final footsteps before execution. It is named after Saint James, the patron saint of Spain, the fort stands on the former site of a palisaded fort belonging to Muslim Tagalog Rajah Matanda. The original structure of Fort Santiago have faced destruction during Chinese pirate invasions, leading to its reconstruction with hard stone (Volcanic tuff) from 1590 to 1593. The completed fort featured a castellated design, including a gun platform named after Saint Barbara and curtain walls extending to face the city.

Baluarte de San Andres


The name Baluarte de San Nicolas, derived from the name of the mother church of the Augustinian Recollects, San Nicolas de Tolentino Church (located on the property currently occupied by the Manila Bulletin), was given to the street named after St. Andrew, the patron saint of Manila. It was constructed in 1603 to prevent land invasions on the southeast side of the walled city and to guard the original Puerta Real, which is now the Gen. Luna Gap. Baluarte de San Andres was refurbished in 1733, when a garita (sentry box or turret) and a bombproof powder magazine were added. Later, a dock was added to allow boats to pass through the moat. But it wasn't rebuilt until after the British occupation, having been destroyed during the invasion. The Intramuros Administration rebuilt it after it was devastated once more in 1945.

Baluarte de San Diego


Baluarte de San Diego, situated in Intramuros, Manila, symbolizes Spanish colonial architecture's defensive prowess, established in 1586. Marked by distinctive bastions, it played a crucial role in safeguarding the city during the British occupation in 1762-1764. The fort's sophisticated military engineering is reflected in its design. Ongoing restoration efforts have preserved it as a cultural heritage site, allowing visitors to delve into its history and appreciate its enduring significance. The fort's origins trace back to Governor General Gómez Pérez Dasmariñas, integrating an older fort with curtain walls in 1591-1594, featuring a bastion designed for enhanced artillery visibility.

San Agustin Church


San Agustin Church, also known as The Church of Saint Augustine, is a Roman Catholic church in Manila, Philippines, situated within the historic walled city of Intramuros. Affiliated with the Order of Saint Augustine, it goes by various names, including the Archdiocesan Shrine of Our Lady of Consolation and Cincture or the Immaculate Conception Parish. Constructed by Augustinian friars in 1587, it holds the distinction of being the oldest stone church in the country. Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993, the church features Baroque architecture with intricate details reflecting Spanish colonial influence. Despite enduring natural disasters and wars, San Agustin Church has preserved its original structure, symbolizing resilience. The interior showcases beautiful frescoes, woodwork, and religious artifacts, providing a historical narrative spanning centuries. Serving as both a religious sanctuary and cultural heritage site, San Agustin Church stands as a testament to the enduring legacy of Spanish colonial architecture.

Ayuntamiento de Manila


The Ayuntamiento de Manila (Manila City Hall) is a building located in Intramuros, Manila. It is also known as the Casas Consistoriales, and also nicknamed as the Marble Palace. The Ayuntamiento was the seat of the Manila City Council, which consisted of two alcaldes (city leaders), eight oidores (judges), a clerk, and a chief constable. In way back to 1599, It is initially serving as a town hall during Spanish colonial rule, played a significant role in hosting key events such as Jose Rizal's execution. It was damaged in World War II but fortunately, it was later reconstructed with neoclassical architecture. Serving as a political and social center, it played a pivotal role in Spanish authority. Post-independence, it had various uses, including as a post office, before restoration highlighted its historical importance. Today, known as the Marble Palace, it houses the Bureau of the Treasury after being destroyed and reconstructed following World War II.

Casa Manila


Casa Manila is a museum in Intramuros, Manila that tends to replicate an 1850s San Nicolas House where was once located in Calle Jaboneros, and was designed by Architect J. Ramon L. Faustmann. It is a Spanish colonial house and was commissioned by Imelda Marcos in the 1980s. The museum aims to showcase the luxurious lifestyle of the Spanish colonial elite during the 19th century, featuring a well-preserved collection of antique furniture, artwork, and artifacts. The architecture reflects colonial influences, with courtyards, balconies, and detailed woodwork. It is located across from the historic San Agustin church which it is part of Barrio San Luis and one of the original villages of Intramuros. It stands alongside other grand houses in the area, such as Los Hidalgos and Cuyugan Mansion.

Manila cathedral


The Mother of all Churches, Cathedrals and Basilicas of the Philippines. The Manila Cathedral-Basilica is the Premier Church of the Philippines because of all the Churches in the archipelago, it was the one chosen to become the Cathedral in 1581 when the Philippines was separated from the Archdiocese of Mexico and became a new diocese with its episcopal seat in Manila. As the Church of Manila, it was established by the secular priest Juan de Vivero in 1571 under the patronage of Mary, La Purissima e Inmaculada Concepcion. Fr. Vivero was given the special privilege and sole faculty by the Archbishop of Mexico to take care of the spiritual welfare of the new Philippine colony.

The Queen Isabel II Statue


(Filipino: Bantayog ni Reyna Isabel II or Monumento ni Reyna Isabel II; Spanish: Monumento a la Reina Isabel II) is located in front of Puerta Isabel II in Intramuros, Manila, Philippines. It is made of bronze and was funded by donations collected from the city in 1854 and 1855. Ponciano Ponzano, a Spanish sculptor was commissioned to do the work. It was erected amidst festive ceremonies on 14 July 1860 near Teatro Alfonso XII in Arroceros in what is now Plaza Lawton (now Liwasang Bonifacio) Political upheavals in Spain led to the downfall of the Queen Isabel II and the rise of the liberal government in 1868. One of its officials, Carlos María de la Torre, was appointed governor-general of the Philippines. It was not long before he set on removing the remnants of the old regime in Manila.

Sta. Lucia barracks


It was constructed for the Artillería de Montaña in 1781 following the plans of Tomas Sanz. Its construction was executed during the governorship of José Basco y Vargas. In 1901, the Philippine Constabulary used the buildings as its headquarters. In 1905, it was opened as a military school, or the Philippine Military Academy in Baguio at present. The building turned into ruins during World War II, thus, the remaining walls were reconstructed by the Philippine Constabulary. The interior was never rebuilt and is being converted into a park.On this site, Cuartel de la Artilleria de Montana was built by Thomas Sanz, Engineer of the army during the Administration of Governor General Jose Basco y Vargas, 1781.

Gates of intramuros


The gates of Intramuros refer to the original eight gates of the Walled City of Intramuros in Manila, built during the Spanish colonial era in the Philippines. The gates are called by the original Spanish word for "gate", puerta (plural: puertas).This gate was built in 1662 as the governor-general's gate when the first governor's palace was still located in Fort Santiago. It was destroyed during an earthquake and was never rebuilt.

Museo de Intramuros


The Museo de Intramuros comprises two important reconstructions: the San Ignacio Church and the Mission House of the Society of Jesus. As the name denotes, the complex now houses the vast ecclesiastical collection of the Intramuros Administration. First built in 1878 by the Society of Jesus and completed in 1889, the San Ignacio Church, according to contemporaries, was said to be one of the most beautiful in old Manila. Miller (1906), who called it the “jewel of Intramuros,” described its interior as “ravishingly beautiful,” Kemlein (1908) stated that “the charming splendor of this church will never fail to make the deepest impression upon the visitor,” while O’Reilly (1940) wrote that “nowhere in the Islands will more excellent wood carvings be found than here.” The church was the masterpiece of old Manila’s greatest artists. Don Felix Roxas, reputedly the most famous architect of the day, designed the structure, while grandmasters such as Isabelo Tampinco, Crispulo Hocson, Agustin Saez, and Manuel Flores (wood-works), Francisco Rodoreda (marble-works), and Hilario Sunico (metal-works) painstakingly decorated the interior and exterior of the building.

Palacio Arzopisbal


The Palacio Arzobispal is the residence of the Roman Catholic Archbishop of Manila located in Intramuros, Manila. The Archiepiscopal Palace of Manila was established as one of the five episcopal palaces in the Philippines.Here once stood the Archbishop's Palace. Acquired by Archbishop Miguel de Poblete (1653-1667) for use as residence. Housed the offices of the Archbishop of Manila and the Catholic Church in the Philippines. Underwent major repairs after damage from earthquakes. Destroyed in 1944. Present building constructed in the 1980's for the offices of the Arzobispado de Manila (Archdiocese of Manila).

Bahay Tsinoy


"Bahay" meaning house, and "Tsinoy" colloquially referring to a Filipino of Chinese descent, Bahay Tsinoy in Intramuros, Manila, stands as a cultural repository. This museum celebrates and preserves the intertwined history and contributions of the Chinese community in the Philippines. Through exhibits featuring artifacts, documents, and multimedia presentations, it serves as a vital bridge, fostering understanding and appreciation for the shared heritage between Filipino and Chinese cultures.

Benavides Monument


Dedicated to Miguel Benavides, a Spanish Dominican friar, this monument in front of the Manila Cathedral honors a key figure in Philippine history. Miguel Benavides played a crucial role in the establishment of the University of Santo Tomas, Asia's oldest university. Reflecting historical ties between the Philippines and Spain, the monument stands as a tribute to Benavides' contributions to education and the Dominican Order.

Palacio del Gobernador


Translating to "Palace of the Governor," this colonial-era government building within Intramuros, Manila, carries a rich history. Initially a residence for Spanish governors, it later served as the seat of the colonial government. Blending Spanish and Mexican influences in its architecture, the Palacio del Gobernador symbolizes the Philippines' colonial past and stands as a historical landmark within the walls of Intramuros.

National Shrine of Our Lady of Lourdes


Situated in Quezon City, this revered Catholic church dedicated to the Virgin Mary is the National Shrine of Our Lady of Lourdes. Drawing devotees seeking solace and inspiration, the shrine's architecture and serene surroundings create a contemplative atmosphere. Rooted in the Marian apparitions in Lourdes, France, the church stands as a place of prayer, reflection, and religious pilgrimage within the bustling city.
